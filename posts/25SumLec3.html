Abstract

A wide type of cotorsion pairs are constructed via covering and enveloping. We analysis the existence of projective cover at the beginning, and see where the obstakle rises. Then we introduce criterion of the existence of cover/envelope.

All Covers are Projective Covers

We explain the general theory of (pre)covers with projective (pre)covers in a functorial approach.

(Small/superfluous). A subobject $i : K ↪ X$ is small, if for any subobject $j : L ↪ X$ with $L + K = X$, then $j$ must be an isomorphism.

Say $p : P → M$ is a projective cover, if the following equivalent conditions hold (projective cover = $𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣$-cover!):

  1. (Projective cover). $p$ is an epimorphism, and $\ker p$ (as a morphism) is small;
  2. ($𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣$-cover). Any morphism $Q → M$ ($Q$ projective) factors through $p$, and any $p ∘ α = p$ imples $α$ is an automorphism.

(1 → 2). Clearly, $Q → M$ factors through $p$ by lifting property of projectives. We show that $p ∘ α = p$ implies $α$ is an automorphism. We construct the pullback square of monomorphisms:



By $p ∘ α = p$, the morphism $p|_{\operatorname{im}α }$ is epic, thus the first row is an ses. By exercise, the subobject $\operatorname{im}(α ) ↪ P$ is an isomorphism. This shows $α$ is epic. We show $α$ is also monic. Let $j$ be the right inverse of $α$ (by lifting property of projectives). Then $p ∘ j = p ∘ α ∘ j = p$. Hence $j$ is epic. Since $j$ is also splic monic, $j$ is an isomorphism. Therefore $α$ is an automorphism.
(2 → 1). $p$ is epic, since epimrophism $Q ↠ M$ factors through $p$. We show $\ker p$ is small. If not, there exists proper subobject $N ↪ P$ s.t. $N + \ker p = P$. Therefore we have the pullback pushout square of ses’s. We introduce $l$ by lifting property of projectives, then $α : λ ∘ l$ is a non-automorphism such that $p ∘ α = p$, a contradiction.



Here we do not require the objects are well-powered, which means that all subobjects of a certain object form a set.

Functorial Approach

The Yoneda embedding $\mathcal{A} ↪ 𝐏𝐒𝐡 (\mathcal{A})$ via $X ↦ (-, X)_\mathcal{A}$ is fully faithful and left exact. In particular, the representable functor $(-, X)_\mathcal{A}$ are projective in $𝐏𝐒𝐡 (\mathcal{A})$.

The Yoneda embedding gives a bijection $(X,Y) ↔ [(-, X), (-, Y)]_{𝐏𝐒𝐡 (\mathcal{A})}$, which indentifies $f$ with a collection of natural transformation $\{(A, f)\}_{A ∈ \mathcal{A}}$.

  • The assignment is $? ↦ (-, ?)$ preserves all limits, thus is left exact;
  • For any surjection $θ : F → G$ of presheaves, we see $[(-, ?), θ_-] ≃ θ_?$ is surjective. Hence, representable functors are projective in $𝐏𝐒𝐡 (\mathcal{A})$.

Now we gives a functorial characterisation of (pre)cover. Throughout, $\mathcal{F}$ is a replete (= closed under iso.) class of objects.

((Pre)cover). Let $X ∈ \mathcal{X}$ and $F ∈ \mathcal{F}$. Say $f : F → X$ is a $\mathcal{F}$-precover, provided the following equivalent conditions:

  1. Any morphism $g : G → X$ with $G ∈ \mathcal{F}$ factors through $f$;
  2. There is an epimorphism $f_∗ : (- , F)_\mathcal{F} ↠ (-, X)_\mathcal{A}$ from the projective object $(-, F)_\mathcal{F} ∈ 𝐏𝐒𝐡 (\mathcal{F})$.

Say a precover $f : F → X$ is a cover, provided the following equivalent conditions:

  1. Any endomorphism $α : F → X$ such that $f ∘ α = f$ implies $α$ is an automorphism;
  2. The epimorphism $f_∗ : (-, F)_\mathcal{F} ↠ (-, X)_\mathcal{A}$ is a projective cover.

Note that for $2$, $\mathrm{End}_\mathcal{F}(F) ≃ \mathrm{End}_{𝐏𝐒𝐡 (\mathcal{F})}((-, F)_\mathcal{F})$ via $α ↦ α _∗$.

Categorically speaking, to study the cover, it suffices to study the projective covers.

The functorial construction has amazing results for AR theory, e.g., this note, or a general theory of Auslander algebra.

A Tautological Comparison

We learn from the above that

  • either projective precovers or covers are epic;
  • if $M$ admits a projective cover, then it is unique under isomorphism, and is always a summand of $Q ↠ M$;
  • $p : P ↠ M$ is a (pre)cover whenever $p_∗ : (-, P)_{𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣} ↠ (-|_{𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣}, M)_{\mathcal{A}}$ is.

We are glad to compare the projective covers with that of the functorial cases.

(Idempotent obstacle). There is the case that idempotent morphism $e : X → X$ does not split. In this case $\mathrm{im}(-, e)$ is a non-representable f.g. projective objects.

Assuming $\mathcal{A}$ is idempotent complete, we begin with a well-known lemma.

(Special Yoneda lemma). This is an element result in representation theory. Let $T'$ be a summand of finite biproducts of $T$. There is an isomorphism

$$\begin{equation} (T', M)_{\mathcal{A}} → ((T,T')_\mathcal{A}, (T, M)_\mathcal{A})_{\mathrm{End}(T)}, \quad f ↦ f_∗ . \end{equation}$$

The right module structure of $(T,M)$ is given by $f ⋅ α = f ∘ α ^∗$ for $α ∈ \mathrm{End}(T)$.

(Projectivisation). Let $𝐚𝐝𝐝 (T)$ denote the full subcategory contains $T$ and closed under isomorphisms, finite biproducts and summands. Then there is an equivalence:

$$\begin{equation} (T,-) : 𝐚𝐝𝐝 (T) → 𝐩𝐫𝐨𝐣 (𝐌𝐨𝐝 _{\mathrm{End}(T)}),\quad T_0 ↦ (T, T_0). \end{equation}$$

By special Yoneda lemma.

The tautological comparison is as follows.

We assume idempotent completeness. Let $P ↠ M$ be an epimorphism from projectives, then the following are equivalent.

  1. $p : P ↠ M$ admits a projective cover;
  2. $p_∗ : (-, P)_{𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣} ↠ (-|_{𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣}, M)$ admits a projective cover;
  3. $p_∗ : (-, P)_{𝐚𝐝𝐝 (P)} ↠ (-|_{𝐚𝐝𝐝 (P)}, M)$ admits a projective precover;
  4. $p_∗ : (P, P)_{\mathrm{End}(P)} ↠ (P, M)_{\mathrm{End}(P)}$ admits a projective cover.

Note that all these are precovers as they are epimorphisms.

(1 → 2 → 3) is clear, (3 → 4) is given by special Yoneda lemma. It suffices to show (4 → 1).
When $p_∗ : (P, P)_{\mathrm{End}(P)} ↠ (P, M)_{\mathrm{End}(P)}$ admits a projective cover, it takes the form

$$\begin{equation} (P, P)_{\mathrm{End}(P)} ↠ Q ↠ (P, M)_{\mathrm{End}(P)}. \end{equation}$$

Note that $Q ≃ (P, P_0)_{\mathrm{End}(P)}$ for $P_0 ∈ 𝐒𝐦𝐝 (P)$ by projectivisation. With special Yoneda lemma, we can write the mapping sequence as

$$\begin{equation} (P, P)_{\mathrm{End}(P)} \overset {a_∗ } ↠ (P, P_0)_{\mathrm{End}(P)} \overset {b_∗ } ↠ (P, M)_{\mathrm{End}(P)}. \end{equation}$$

We show that $b : P_0 → M$ is a projective cover. By special Yoneda lemma, $(ba)_∗ = π _∗$ yields $ba = π$. Hence $b$ is epic. When there is $b ∘ γ = b$, we have $b_∗ ∘ γ _∗ = b_∗$. Now $γ _∗$ is an isomorphism as $b_∗$ is a projective cover. By projectivisation, $γ$ is an automorphism.

It is a local property for exhibiting a projective cover. For idempotent completed cases, $P ↠ M$ has a projective cover when $\mathrm{End}(P)$ is right perfect.

Torsion Theory

The covering need not be epimorphisms! A trivial example is that every additive category admits a $\{0\}$-covering. A general kind of examples is known as the torsion theory.

Show that every f.g. Abelian group is a unique direct sum of finite Abelian groups and a f.g. free group.

Let $t : 𝐚𝐛 → 𝐚𝐛^{\text{finite}}$ be the functor of taking maximal torsion subgroup. Then $t(M) ↪ M$ is an $𝐚𝐛^{\text{finite}}$-cover.

The f.g. torsion groups ($𝐚𝐛^{\text{finite}}$) and f.g. free Abelian groups ($𝐚𝐛^{\text{f.g. free}}$) are orthogonal with respect to $\mathrm{Hom}$-functor. We write $X ⟂_o Y$ for $\mathrm{Hom}(X,Y) = 0$, and denote $(-)^o := (-)^{⟂_o}$ and $^o(-) := {}^{⟂_o}(-)$ for simplicity.

Unwinding a brief introduction, we show enumerate some closure properties of a torsion class as follows.

(Torsion theory). Let $\mathcal{A}$ be an Abelian category which is well-powered and subobjects are closed under transfinite filtrations. Then the following are equivalent for defining a torsion theory $(\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{F})$:

  1. There is a subfunctor $t ⊆ 1_\mathcal{A}$ s.t. $t ∘ t = t$ and $t(\frac{M}{tM}) = 0$. Here $t ∘ t = t$ means that $t(tM) = tM$ when idenfitying subobjects of $tM$ as that of $M$. Set $\mathcal{T} = \operatorname{im} t$ and $\mathcal{F} = \ker t$.
  2. $\mathcal{T}⟂ _o \mathcal{F}$, and every object $M$ fits into a ses $0 → tM → M → M/tM → 0$. In this case, such ses is functorial in $M$.
  3. $\mathcal{T}^o = \mathcal{F}$ and $\mathcal{T} = {}^o \mathcal{F}$, or other equivalent form given by Galois connection.
  4. $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{T}^o$, and $\mathcal{T}$ is closed under taking transfinite filtration (including extensions) and quotient objects.
  5. $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{T}^o$, and there is a class of objects $\mathcal{X}$ such that $\mathcal{T}$ consists of objects which are transfinite filtration of quotient objects in $\mathcal{X}$.

We explain 1 ↔ 2 → 3 → 4 ↔ 5 in few wrods, and show 5 → 1 (which requires supplementary conditions).
(1 ↔ 2). A canonical injection refers to a canonical ses $t$. (2 → 3). For instance, we show $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{T} ^o$. We claim $\mathcal{T} ⟂ _o \mathcal{F}$, if there is non-zero $f : T → F$, then $\operatorname{im}(f) ∈ \mathcal{T}$ is a subobject of $F$, yielding $tF ≠ 0$. Conversely we show $\mathcal{T} ^o ⊆ \mathcal{F}$, notice that every $X ∈ \mathcal{T} ^o$ has no subobjects in $\mathcal{T} ∖ \{0\}$. (3 → 4). Note that any $^o\mathcal{X}$ is closed under extensions and quotients. (4 ↔ 5). Clearly $→$ is by taking $\mathcal{X} = \mathcal{T}$. Conversely, any particular class $𝐅𝐢𝐥 (\text{quotients of } \ \mathcal{X})$ is closed under transfinite filtrations and quotient objects.
(4 → 2). We show $X ∈ \mathcal{A}$ has a maximal subobject in $\mathcal{T}$, which is also the greatest. Let $\mathcal{S}$ denote the set of subobjects of $X$ lying in $\mathcal{T}$. $0 ∈ \mathcal{S}$ is non-empty. Notice that,

  • Every chain in $\mathcal{S}$ has an upper bound, as $\mathcal{T}$ is closed under transfinite filtrations.
  • Any two maximal objects $T_1, T_2 ∈ \mathcal{S}$ coincides. If not, then $(T_1 + T_2) ∈ \mathcal{T}$, a contradiction.

Let $tX$ denote such maximal subobject in $\mathcal{T}$, we complete the proof.

We call $\mathcal{T}$ a torsion class, and $\mathcal{F}$ a torsion-free class.

The above are equivalent for either $𝐌𝐨𝐝 _R$ or $C(R)$. Note that these categories are well-powered and cocompleted.

  2. $i_∙ : M_∙ ↪ X$ induces a monomorphism $\varinjlim ^{fil} M_∙ ↪ X$.

Envelopes

This is the dual statement of covers. The general theory of envelopes also translated to that of projective covers.

Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a replete class of objects. For any $E ∈ \mathcal{E}$, say $j : X ↪ E$ is

  1. a preenvelope, provided an epimorphism from projective objects $j ^∗ : (E, -)_\mathcal{E} ↠ (X, -)_{\mathcal{A}}$;
  2. an envelope, provided the projective cover $j ^∗ : (E, -)_\mathcal{E} ↠ (X, -)_{\mathcal{A}}$.

There might (the writer never met it before) be a dual concept called co-small quotient module or co-superfluous quotient module. Say $p: X ↠ C$ is co-small, provided for any quotient object $q: X ↠ C'$ such that $X \xrightarrow ∼ C' × _{C ⊔ _X C'} C$, $q$ is an isomorphism.

For set-theoritic consideration, we substitute the expression co-small with essnetial extension.

$p : X ↠ C$ is co-small, iff $\ker p ↪ X$ is a essential extension which is defined as follows:

  • any non-zero submodule $L ↪ X$ has a non-zero intersection with $\ker p$.

(→). Suppose $p : X ↠ C$ is co-small, while there exists a non-zero submodule $L ↪ X$ s.t. $L ∩ \ker p = 0$. Then we shall see contradictions. By definition, one as the following pushout-pullback square



By definition of co-small, $X = \frac{X}{L \cap \ker p} ↠ \frac{X}{L}$ is an isomorphism, yieling $L = 0$, a contradiction.
(←). Conversely, suppose that $\ker p ↪ X$ is essential yet $p : X ↠ \frac{X}{\ker p}$ is not co-small. Then it is possible to find a non-zero submodule $L$ such that the above diagram is a pushout-pullback square. Now $L ∩ \ker p = 0$, a contradiction.

Show $i : X → E$ is an injective envelope, provided the following equivalent conditions:

  1. $i$ is injective with a co-small cokernel;
  2. $i ^∗ : (E, -)_\mathcal{E} ↠ (X, -)_{\mathcal{A}}$ is a projective cover.

Unlike projective covers, the injective envelopes always exists.

Every module $X ∈ 𝐌𝐨𝐝 _R$ has an injective envelope.

Set $\mathcal{S} : \{i : X ↪ Y ∣ i \ \text{is essential}\}$. We claim every ascending chain in $\mathcal{S}$ has an upper bound. Note that

$$\begin{equation} ⋃ _{i ∈ I} (L ∩ Y_i) = L ∩ (⋃_{i ∈ I} Y_i)\qquad (⇔ \text{AB5 condition}). \end{equation}$$

We take any maximal object $Q ∈ \mathcal{S}$. Clearly $Q$ has only trivial essential extensions. We claim that $Q$ is injective by showing any ses $0 → Q → E → E/Q → 0$ splits. Again by AB5, there is a maximal subobject in $T ↪ E$ s.t. $T ∩ Q = 0$. By definition, $Q ↪ \frac{E}{T}$ is essential, thus $Q ↪ E ↠ \frac{E}{T}$ is an isomorphism. Now $Q+T = E$ and $Q ∩ T = 0$, hence $E = Q ⊕ T$.
Finally, $X ↪ Q$ is an injective envelope as it is monic with co-small cokernel (= essential extension).

We provide an non-trivial example that projective cover does not exists, yet the injective envelope is easy to construct. We begin with a well-known theorem.

(Specker’s theorem). $\mathrm{Hom}(∏_ω ℤ , ℤ ) ≃ ∐_ω ℤ$.

We show that any $f : ∏_ω ℤ → ℤ$ vanishes in the ultraproduct part, that is, $f = 0$ if $f|_{∐_ω ℤ } = 0$.

  • Suppose $f|_{∐_ω ℤ } = 0$, but there is some infinite sequence $(x_n)$ s.t. $A := f((x_n)) ≠ 0$. There is a way to write $x_n = 2^n ⋅ b_n+ 3^n ⋅ c_n$. Set $B := f((2^n ⋅ b_n))$ and $C := f((3^n ⋅ c_n))$. Now $A = B + C$. By cancelling the first finite terms, we see $2^n ∣ B$ for any $n$. Hence $B = 0$. Similarly $C = 0$. Now $A = 0$.

It suffices to show $f(e_k)$ vanishes for some large $k$. If not, then we assume (WLOG) every $f(e_k) ≠ 0$. We construct a sharply increasing sequence $s := (2^{n_0}, 2^{n_1}, \ldots )$, so that $|f(s_{≤ k})| ≪ n_{k+1}$ for $k ≥ 1$. Now we have triangle inequality

$$\begin{equation} |f(s)| + |f(s_{≤ k})| ≥ |f((s_{≥ k+1}))|\quad (∀ k). \end{equation}$$

Since $f(s)$ is constant, and $\lim_{k → ∞ }\frac{|f(s_{≤ k})|}{|f((s_{≥ k+1}))|} = 0$, a contradiction.

We show a non-trivial example of module with injective envelope but not projective cover. Take $R = ℤ$ which is PID (hence coherent) set $X = ℤ^ω$.

  1. $X$ is flat, since flatness are preserved by products over coherent rings.
  2. $X$ is not projective (= free over PID). If there is $ℤ ^{(λ)} ≃ ℤ^ω $, then $ℤ ^λ ≃ \mathrm{Hom}(ℤ^{(λ)}, ℤ ) ≃ \mathrm{Hom}(ℤ^ω, ℤ ) ≃ ℤ^{(ω)}$. Since $λ$ is uncountable, we see the contradiction.
  3. $X$ has a injective envelope $ℤ ^ω ↪ ℚ ^ω$. $ℚ ^ω$ is clearly injective. Since $ℤ ↪ ℚ$ is essential, $ℤ ^ω ↪ ℚ ^ω$ is also essential.

Flat Covers and Its Cotorsion Theory

Flat Cotorsion Pair

Flat modules are closed under transfinite filtrations.

We prove with transfinite induction. Suppose $\{M_γ\}_{γ < α}$ is a transfinite filtration of flat modules.

  1. $M_0 = 0$ is flat.
  2. If $M_γ$ and $M_{γ + 1} / M_γ$ are flat, then the extension $M_{γ + 1}$ is also flat (by taking $\mathrm{Tor}_1$).
  3. If $\{M_{β}\}_{γ < β}$ are flat for some limit ordinal $β$, then for any ses $θ$, the filtered colimit of ses $\varinjlim^{fil}_{γ < β} (M_γ ⊗ θ)$ is also a ses.

It is straightforward to show that $(𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭 , 𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭 ^⟂ )$ is a cotorsion theory. Since

  • $𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣 ⊆ 𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭$;
  • $𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭$ is closed under taking summands;
  • $𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭$ is closed under transfinite filtrations.

Hence, $𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭 = 𝐒𝐦𝐝 (𝐏𝐫𝐨𝐣 ∗ 𝐅𝐢𝐥 (𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭)) = {}^⟂ (𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭^⟂ )$.

We call $(𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭 , 𝐅𝐥𝐚𝐭 ^⟂)$ a flat cotorsion theory.
